# Yingshi Jufeng Copywriter

Use this skill to write new Chinese video scripts in the style captured below. Treat the style card as a reusable writing system, not as source material to quote. Do not claim to be the original creator, imply endorsement, or copy long phrases from reference videos.

## Inputs

Minimum viable input: a topic, question, product name, trend, or rough direction. An outline is optional.

If the user does not provide every field, make reasonable assumptions and state them briefly:

- Topic or core claim
- Optional outline or key points
- Target audience
- Target length or platform
- Desired intensity, such as sharper, warmer, denser, or more conversational

Default assumptions when unspecified:

- Chinese spoken video script.
- 6 to 10 minute explainer or documentary-style video.
- Audience: curious general viewers with some interest in the subject.
- Goal: make the viewer understand why this topic matters, not just what happened.

## Research-First Mode

Use this mode whenever the user gives only a topic, question, product, market direction, or a time-sensitive subject. Do the research before drafting.

Research targets:

- Current status: launch date, availability, price, model names, official claims, and what is still uncertain.
- Core facts: specs, configuration, mechanism, use cases, and constraints.
- Comparison set: predecessor, direct rivals, adjacent alternatives, and one or two broader reference points when useful.
- Viewer stakes: why an ordinary viewer or potential buyer should care.
- Bigger question: what this case says about the industry, creators, consumers, or technology.

Source rules:

- For specs, prices, release status, and official positioning, prefer manufacturer pages, launch materials, verified official accounts, filings, or reputable media reports.
- Use forums, social platforms, short videos, and comments only for sentiment, questions, and user pain points; do not treat them as hard facts without confirmation.
- For current or upcoming products, browse the web and compare source dates. Do not rely on memory.
- If sources conflict, say so briefly and build the script around the uncertainty instead of hiding it.
- Do not invent numbers, prices, release dates, weights, horsepower, torque, battery specs, or fuel specs.
- Include a compact source note with the final answer when web research was used.

Research-to-script workflow:

1. Turn the user direction into one central viewer question.
2. Build a short research brief: what is known, what is interesting, what is uncertain, what comparisons matter.
3. Choose the style angle: object-first, scene-first, problem-first, or contradiction-first.
4. Create the segment plan before drafting.
5. Draft the script in style, keeping research facts visible but not list-like.

## Writing Workflow

1. Infer the video's viewer promise and emotional payoff from the topic or research brief.
2. Build a segment plan that follows the style card's structure pattern.
3. Draft the script in spoken Chinese with readable paragraph breaks.
4. Preserve the style's rhythm and rhetorical habits while adapting facts to the new topic.
5. Add title suggestions, segment structure, source notes when researched, and a short style self-check.

## Default Output

1. Title suggestions
2. Full script
3. Segment structure
4. Source notes, when research was used
5. Style self-check

## Style Card

# Yingshi Jufeng Copywriting Style Card

Sampling basis: distilled from 15 public Mediastorm videos with subtitle text. The generated skill should stay reusable without the original URLs, subtitle files, or transcripts.

## Style Identity
- Best for: video tech, camera and gear reviews, creator interviews, behind-the-scenes documentaries, sample-footage diaries, socially aware tech commentary, and knowledge-led scripts with strong visual imagination.
- Not for: empty outrage, low-information ranting, legal-medical-financial advice, or any attempt to impersonate the creator as a real person.
- Audience: Chinese-speaking internet viewers who like technology, filmmaking, creativity, industry change, and well-packaged explanations. The style should work for both informed viewers and curious newcomers.

## Opening Pattern
Start with a concrete object, scene, question, or sharp contrast. Then widen it into a larger question worth watching.

Common opening moves:
- Show the viewer something specific, then ask what it really means.
- Open with a slightly absurd or irresistible question.
- Drop the audience into a location or situation before explaining why it matters.
- Within 15 to 30 seconds, make clear what this episode is about and why it is worth staying for.

## Structure Pattern
Typical structure:

Hook -> establish the scene or object -> define the real question -> experience, interview, experiment, or field visit -> explain the mechanism -> lift toward a bigger observation or value judgment -> close with a takeaway

Shared structural habits:
- Do not open with dry conclusions. Bring the viewer into the problem first.
- Do not jump straight into specs. Start with experience, consequence, or meaning, then explain the technical side.
- Keep switching between lived experience and analysis.
- If there is an interview or visit, weave host reaction, guest perspective, and on-site detail together instead of flattening them into transcript blocks.

## Voice And Tone
- Sound like a skilled creator, a curious field reporter, and a friend who can make hard things understandable.
- Stay warm, excited, and sincere without sounding preachy or theatrical.
- Be optimistic about technology, but keep real-world judgment. Do not worship novelty.
- Use "we" often. The voice should feel like a creative team, not a lone pundit.
- Address the viewer directly with "you" when useful so the script feels inviting and companionable.

## Sentence Rhythm
- Prefer spoken-language Chinese with short and medium-length lines.
- Push one idea at a time.
- Use rhythm words like "then", "so", "actually", "but the interesting part is", "you start to notice", and "I think" in natural moderation.
- Build momentum with contrast, rhetorical questions, and small pauses.
- Read like a host script, not like an essay and not like disposable short-video chatter.

## Rhetorical Habits
- Move from a tangible detail to an abstract conclusion.
- Use "you might think A, but the real issue is B" turns.
- Explain technology in human terms first, mechanism second, consequence third.
- Place each case inside a larger frame such as creative method, industry structure, or social change.
- Praise with sincerity, criticize with reasons and boundaries, and avoid empty mockery.
- If brand integration appears, place the product inside a real use case rather than switching into ad copy.

## Ending Pattern
- Pull back from the object or event toward a wider feeling, implication, or belief.
- In documentary and interview pieces, end with a little aftertaste: what this story lets us see.
- In review and commentary pieces, end with what the change means for creators or ordinary people.
- In regular episode form, a light call to action is acceptable, but it should feel gentle and familiar.

## Must Imitate
- Open with something visual, tactile, or intriguing instead of abstract topic statements.
- Tell the viewer early why the episode is worth watching.
- Chain experience, observation, explanation, and judgment together.
- Make technical explanation serve lived experience and real consequence.
- Preserve a creator-team point of view, often through "we".
- Keep the energy real and evidence-backed, not slogan-heavy.
- Use concrete detail to earn the right to make bigger claims.
- Allow light humor and play, but never lose the main line.
- Write brand or product moments as part of the story world.
- End with a slight lift so the viewer feels they understood something larger.

## Avoid
- Do not mimic only the clicky question titles while leaving the body flat.
- Do not write plain spec sheets, plain interview transcripts, or plain brochure copy.
- Do not keep the same emotional pitch all the way through.
- Do not force purple prose or fake sophistication.
- Do not claim to be Yingshi Jufeng or Tim.
- Do not make unverified industry claims sound absolute.
- Do not turn brand moments into stiff ad reads.
- Do not write huge dense blocks that are awkward to read aloud.

## Default Output
- 3 to 5 title options
- full spoken script
- segment structure with purpose of each part
- short style self-check that explains what matched the style and what was intentionally not copied

## Guardrails

- Keep factual claims grounded. If a claim is uncertain, soften it or flag it.
- Do not include full transcript excerpts from reference videos.
- Do not imitate harmful, deceptive, hateful, or privacy-invasive behavior.
- Do not present the generated script as the original creator's words.
